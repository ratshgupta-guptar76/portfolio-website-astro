Throughout history all living beings have had an inherent drive to maintain health and avoid discomfort. Animals, including humans, naturally seek out resources like food, water, and shelter to survive and thrive. This instinct can be observed across species — whether through migration to escape harsh weather or seeking essential resources, there is a fundamental evolutionary need to survive and stay healthy.

So, what is health, and what does being healthy mean? According to the World Health Organization (WHO):

> "Health is a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity."

## Understanding Mental Health

One in seven adolescents suffers from mental health issues, yet these problems often go overlooked. A common myth, unfortunately believed by many, is that mental health issues aren't "real" problems. I don't blame people for thinking this way — mental health issues often have no visible physical symptoms. Disorders like anxiety, paranoia, PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder) and schizophrenia can make someone appear "fine" physically while suffering psychologically.

Mental illnesses like agoraphobia — the intense fear of being in situations where escape might be difficult or help is unavailable — may seem like an overreaction to some, but they are very real and impactful for those affected.

## Why Mental Illnesses Occur

The term "mental" originates from the Latin word *mentalis*, which translates to "mind." Mental illness implies there's an issue with the mind, but is it purely abstract, or is there a physical explanation as well?

To understand mental illness, it helps to first understand how the brain works. The brain controls nearly every part of the body, managing our daily functions, emotions, and long-term development through hundreds of thousands of chemical reactions happening daily. These reactions generate signals that travel through neurons — the functional units of the nervous system — to coordinate all activities.

The brain is divided into three main parts:

- **The Brain Stem** controls involuntary actions like breathing and is connected to the spinal cord.
- **The Cerebrum** is responsible for speech, emotions, judgment, problem-solving, and overall thinking. It regulates body temperature and coordination.
- **The Cerebellum** — the "little brain" — handles voluntary muscle movements, balance, and equilibrium.

Mental illness is believed to occur due to miscommunication between neurons, which can result from various factors including chemical imbalances, genetics, and environmental influences. For example, in Alzheimer's disease, memory loss is associated with a toxic build-up of proteins called "plaques" and "tangles" in the brain. These damage neurons and disrupt communication between them, leading to memory loss and cognitive decline.

Mental disorders are not imaginary or exaggerated; they are legitimate illnesses with tangible effects on the brain. In some cases, psychological conditions can cause physical symptoms without a clear medical explanation — a phenomenon historically referred to as "hysteria," now better understood as psychosomatic or conversion disorders. Historical examples of mass hysteria like the Salem Witch Trials or the Tanganyika Laughing Epidemic demonstrate how psychological factors can manifest in both individual and group physical responses.

Understanding the underlying mechanisms can shed light on the broader mental health crisis we face today.

## Today's Mental Health Crisis

In today's world, mental health is especially important for adolescents — a group particularly vulnerable to stress, anxiety, and emotional turmoil. Adolescence is a stage of life filled with major transitions, making teens more susceptible to mental health challenges. Recognizing and addressing these issues is essential to fostering a healthier, more resilient generation.

As someone who experiences anxiety attacks regularly, I understand firsthand how overwhelming these sensations can be. The uncontrollable racing heart, severe vertigo, nausea, and even hallucinations are real, terrifying experiences — yet they are often dismissed as overreactions or attention-seeking behaviour. While such cases do exist in rare instances like in Munchausen's syndrome, most adolescents struggling with mental health issues face very real and valid challenges.

When adolescents cannot openly discuss their mental state, they often internalize their struggles, leading to even more severe conditions like depression or obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD). It's time we start giving mental health the attention it deserves.

## Conclusion

Mental health is a vital component of overall well-being and should never be overlooked. As society continues to dismantle the stigma surrounding mental health disorders, it is crucial to acknowledge the real and profound impact these challenges have on individuals, particularly adolescents. Young people face unique pressures during their formative years, making them especially vulnerable to mental health struggles.

By fostering open conversations and offering support, we can create a more compassionate environment for those in need. Mental health, just like physical health, deserves equal attention and care.
